The Rayleighâ€“Jeans Law predicts that the energy density of electromagnetic radiation in a black body at temperature $T$ is given by:

$$u(\nu, T) = \frac{8\pi \nu^2}{c^3} \cdot \frac{k,T}{h\nu}$$

where $k$ is the Boltzmann constant, $h$ is Planck's constant, and $c$ is the speed of light.

To get the total energy density, we integrate over all frequencies:

$$U(T) = \int_0^\infty u(\nu, T) \, d\nu$$

This integral diverges, leading to the ultraviolet catastrophe. The resolution of this problem was provided by Max Planck in 1900, who proposed that electromagnetic radiation is quantized and can only be emitted or absorbed in discrete packets of energy called quanta or photons.

Planck's law of black body radiation is given by:

$$u(\nu, T) = \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT} - 1}$$

To evaluate:

$$\int_0^\infty \frac{x^3}{e^{x} - 1} \, dx$$

We know that while $x$ is positive, $$\frac{1}{e^{x} - 1}$$ is a fraction less than 1. In other words, for $x > 0$, $$\frac{1}{e^{x} - 1} < 1$$. So, multiplying by $e^{-x}$, we have a term in the form $e/(1-e)$:

$$\frac{e^{-x}}{1-e^{-x}}$$

Which in terms of $r$ is in the form:

$$\frac{1}{1-r}$$

Where $r = e^{-x}$. This is a geometric series, and we can use the formula for the sum of a geometric series:

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty r^n = \frac{1}{1-r}$$

Therefore, we can write:

$$\frac{1}{e^{x} - 1} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty e^{-nx}$$

Substituting this into the integral gives us:

$$\int_0^\infty x^3 \sum_{n=1}^\infty e^{-nx} \, dx$$

We can switch the order of summation and integration to get:

$$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \int_0^\infty x^3 e^{-nx} \, dx$$

The validity of this step comes from the dominated convergence theorem, which states that if a sequence of functions converges pointwise to a function and is dominated by an integrable function, then we can interchange the limit and the integral.

Now we can take a closer look at the integral:

$$\int_0^\infty x^3 e^{-nx} \, dx$$

Using $u$-substitution, let $u = nx$, then $du = n \, dx$ and $dx = \frac{du}{n}$. The limits of integration remain the same as $x$ goes from 0 to $\infty$ as $u$ does. Substituting these into the integral gives us:

$$\int_0^\infty \frac{u^3}{n^4} e^{-u} \, du$$

Or:

$$\frac{1}{n^4} \int_0^\infty u^3 e^{-u} \, du$$

This looks like:

$$\frac{1}{n^4} \Gamma(4)$$

Where $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ is the gamma function.

The gamma function is defined as:

$$\Gamma(n) = \int_0^\infty x^{n-1} e^{-x} \, dx$$

For $n=1$, we have:

$$\Gamma(1) = \int_0^\infty e^{-x} \, dx = 1$$

To prove that $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$, we can prove that $\Gamma(n+1) = n \cdot \Gamma(n)$:

$$\Gamma(n+1) = \int_0^\infty x^n e^{-x} \, dx$$

Using integration by parts, let $u = x^n$ and $dv = e^{-x} \, dx$. Then $du = n x^{n-1} \, dx$ and $v = -e^{-x}$. So we have:

$$\Gamma(n+1) = -x^n e^{-x} \bigg|_0^\infty + n \int_0^\infty x^{n-1} e^{-x} \, dx$$

The first term evaluates to 0 as $x \to \infty$ and $x=0$. So we have:

$$\Gamma(n+1) = n \int_0^\infty x^{n-1} e^{-x} \, dx$$

$$\Gamma(n+1) = n \cdot \Gamma(n)$$

By induction, we can show that $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ for all positive integers $n$. Therefore, we can write:

$$\int_0^\infty x^3 e^{-nx} \, dx = \frac{1}{n^4} \cdot 6$$

Where $\Gamma(4) = 3! = 6$. Now we can substitute this back into the sum:

$$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{6}{n^4}$$

We can factor out the 6 to get:

$$6 \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^4}$$

The Riemann zeta function is defined as:

$$\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s}$$

For $s=4$, we have:

$$\zeta(4) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^4}$$

Using Fourier series and Parseval's Identity, we can derive $\zeta(4)$:

Consider the Fourier series for the function $f(x) = x^2$ on the interval $[-\pi, \pi]$:

$$f(x) = x^2 = \frac{\pi^2}{3} + 4 \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2} \cos(nx)$$

Parseval's Identity states that:

$$\frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^\pi |f(x)|^2 \, dx = a_0^2 + 2 \sum_{n=1}^\infty (a_n^2 + b_n^2)$$

Applying Parseval's Identity to $f(x) = x^2$, we can compute the coefficients and relate them to $\zeta(4)$. After simplifications, we find:

$$\zeta(4) = \frac{\pi^4}{90}$$

This completes the derivation of the Riemann zeta function at $s=4$.

$$\int_0^\infty \frac{x^3}{e^{x} - 1} \, dx = \Gamma(4) \cdot \zeta(4)$$ where $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ and $\zeta(s)$ is the Riemann zeta function. For $s=4$, we have: $$\zeta(4) = \frac{\pi^4}{90}$$ Therefore, we can write: $$\int_0^\infty \frac{x^3}{e^{x} - 1} \, dx = 6 \cdot \frac{\pi^4}{90} = \frac{\pi^4}{15}$$
